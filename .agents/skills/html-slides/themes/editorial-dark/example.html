On restraint.

A meditation on what slides leave out, and why what remains carries the argument.

Editorial dark theme · Specimen deck

I.

The opening.

Every essay-as-deck rests on a single conceit: that the reader will keep reading. The title is a contract. The first paragraph honours it or breaks it. Decoration cannot save a flat argument, and a sharp argument needs little decoration to land.

II.

A theme is a stance.

"The grid is not neutral. The serif is not neutral. The colour you didn't pick is not neutral. A theme is a stance the deck takes before it says a word."

— On editorial decks, 2026
III.

Some numbers, but not too many.

In a thousand-word feature, two numbers earn their place. In a six-slide deck, one chart does. The rest is prose. Forty-eight per cent of the readings collected confirmed the hypothesis; the remaining slides argued why.

IV.

The closing.

End where you started, but with the reader changed. The opening was a contract; the close is its receipt. If the audience can recite a single sentence from the deck a week later, the deck worked.
