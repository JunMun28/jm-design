─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  NARRATIVE ARC
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Core thesis:
    Sales found our first hundred customers.
    The product will find the next thousand.
    The motion is shifting — and the math now agrees.

  Story spine — one question per slide:

    01  TITLE          —  Why are we here?
    02  WHERE WE ARE   —  What did outbound build?
    03  THE STRAIN     —  Why can't we just do more of it?
    04  THE SHIFT      —  What replaces it?
    05  EARLY SIGNAL   —  Does it actually work for us?
    06  Q4 PLAN        —  What changes on Monday?
    07  THE ASK        —  What do we need from the board?

  Run order:
    Linear · 01 → 07. Build to the ask. Hold Q&A until after slide 07.
    Slide 05 is the inflection point — that's where conviction shifts from
    "interesting" to "obvious." Sit on it.


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  DESIGN PRINCIPLES
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Say less, mean more.
    • One idea per slide. Nothing else.
    • Sentences are short. Periods are loud.
    • One italic purple accent word per H2. Never more.
    • Word budget per slide: 25–50 words. Title < 12.

  Hairlines, not cards.
    • A board deck is not a Notion page. We avoid card grids.
    • Use horizontal hairlines to bracket the canvas, vertical hairlines
      to divide content. Whitespace does the column work.
    • Allow ONE soft purple wash for the whole deck — slide 05, the
      moment we want them to feel.
    • Every slide must look visually distinct from its neighbours.

  Card budget for the entire deck: 1.

  Board-deck discipline (additions beyond the reference):
    • Numbers are real or clearly flagged. No vanity stats.
    • Every claim points to a source on the appendix (kept out of the deck).
    • Operators read the verbs. Investors read the math. Both must
      finish each slide in under 20 seconds.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  DESIGN MOVES   (intentional choices for this brainstorm · iteration 1)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Why 7 slides and not more
    – Board attention curves down hard after slide 6. By 7 we want
      the ask, not another argument.
    – Cut from a 10-slide draft: removed a "competitor PLG case
      studies" slide (anecdotal, not our math), a "market timing"
      slide (felt like an investor pitch, not a board update),
      and a separate "risks" slide (folded into stage notes and
      the verbal Q&A prep — risks shouldn't compete with the ask
      for visual weight).

  Why slide 05 is the only card
    – Card budget = 1, matching the reference deck. The pilot data
      IS the moment of the deck. Containing it inside a soft purple
      wash makes the slide feel like a quote pulled out of the
      narrative — which is exactly what it is.

  Why the title slide leads with a sentence pair, not a label
    – "Sales found our first hundred. Product finds the next thousand."
      reads as a thesis the board can repeat in the parking lot.
      "Q3 Board Review: PLG Transition" is a calendar invite — not
      a thesis.

  Why outbound isn't the villain
    – Slide 02 explicitly thanks outbound. Slide 03 is "we can't
      sell harder," not "outbound is broken." The board approved
      outbound — we don't want them to feel they made a mistake.
      We want them to feel the company has earned its next chapter.

  Net result
    – Every slide visually distinct from its neighbours.
    – Whitespace is the design.
    – The deck holds 7 ideas, not 70.
    – Operators and investors finish each slide with the same
      conclusion.
