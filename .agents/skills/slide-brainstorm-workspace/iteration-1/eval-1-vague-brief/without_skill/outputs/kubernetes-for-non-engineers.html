The Problem 03 / 10

The problem: software is unreliable at scale.

Imagine running a restaurant chain with 200 locations.
Each location is a server. Each menu item is an app.

  • A stove breaks → diners go hungry.
  • A franchise gets slammed at 7pm → 40-minute wait.
  • A new menu launches → some locations get the recipe wrong.
  • A chef quits → no one knows what to cook.

The old way

Humans manually move workloads between servers, restart things, scale up, scale down — at 3am, by hand, often wrong.

This doesn’t scale to thousands of services and millions of users.

The Lego Brick 05 / 10

Before Kubernetes, there’s the container.

Think of a container as a self-contained lunchbox holding one app plus everything it needs to run — code, ingredients, instructions.

It runs the same way on your laptop, in the cloud, in someone else’s data center. No surprises.

Kubernetes ≠ Containers

Containers are the lunchboxes. Kubernetes is the cafeteria operator deciding where, when, and how many lunchboxes get served.

You’ll also hear “Docker.” Docker is the most famous lunchbox brand. Kubernetes works with all of them.

Reality check 09 / 10

It’s powerful. It’s also not magic.

Common myths

  • “It makes apps faster.” No — it makes them more resilient.
  • “We need it because we’re modern.” Not always. Small teams can do simpler.
  • “It saves money on day one.” Usually costs more first, then pays back.

Real costs

  • Learning curve. Teams need 3–6 months to be comfortable.
  • Hidden complexity. Networking, security, storage all get harder.
  • Talent premium. Engineers with deep K8s skills are expensive.

Good rule of thumb: if you have < 5 services and < 100K users, the simpler path may serve you better. Ask your engineers honestly.
